Research Proposal UC Berkeley MIDS April 2026

TomoFair

Evaluating algorithmic fairness of AI-assisted digital breast tomosynthesis screening across demographic subgroups.

Black women in the United States die from breast cancer at rates roughly 42% higher than White women — a gap that screening rates alone do not explain. AI-assisted digital breast tomosynthesis is now standard equipment in much of US screening. The question is whether it closes that gap, widens it, or merely moves it elsewhere.

§ 01 The premise

Two true things, in tension.

AI-assisted screening improves aggregate cancer detection at scale. The largest real-world US analysis to date reported a 21% gain over standard mammography. Nationwide German implementation has shown comparable gains; Danish, Korean, and UK deployments have followed.

The same FDA-cleared algorithms have shown elevated false-positive rates in Black patients, with disparities persisting after adjustment for breast density and age. Race-correlated imaging features appear to drive differential algorithmic outputs independently of clinical covariates. Both findings are robust. Both follow from the data.

Current regulatory practice permits clearance on aggregate performance without disaggregated subgroup reporting. Inequities are documented after deployment, not before — which is to say, they are documented in patients.

§ 02 The question

Does AI-assisted breast cancer screening perform equitably across racial and ethnic subgroups?

Measured against a no-AI control, across six performance metrics, disaggregated by FDA-defined demographic strata, in a prospective stratified-random design. The design's contribution is methodological, not algorithmic — an evaluation protocol that produces equity gap scores comparable across vendors, settings, and time periods.

§ 03 Study specifications

Setting
NYC hospitals offering mammographic screening; ≥4 radiologists; no prior AI deployment.
Sample
60,000 patients — 5,000 per cell across six demographic strata × two conditions.
Design
Prospective, randomized, stratified by race / ethnicity per FDA Guidance.
Method
Mixed-effects logistic regression with subgroup × condition interaction terms; equity gap analysis as the primary outcome.
Duration
Five years — three years of enrollment plus two years of outcome follow-up.
Outcome
Six performance metrics, disaggregated by demographic stratum, with cross-strata variance reported as the equity gap.

§ 04 The six metrics

What gets measured, and why.

Each metric is computed for the AI-assisted and control conditions, then disaggregated by patient race. The magnitude of cross-strata variation is the equity gap — the primary unit of fairness.

  1. AUC Overall ability to distinguish cancer from non-cancer cases.
  2. False positive rate Patients flagged as having cancer who do not — the cost is unnecessary biopsy, anxiety, and follow-up.
  3. False negative rate Cancers the system misses. The most consequential error: delays diagnosis and treatment.
  4. Recall Sensitivity to true cancer — proportion of malignancies the system correctly identifies.
  5. True negative rate Specificity — proportion of non-cancer cases correctly cleared.
  6. Precision Reliability of a positive flag — what fraction of alarms are real.

§ 05 The stratified sample

Six strata × two conditions × five thousand.

The lowest expected enrollment in any stratum sets the per-cell baseline. Above-baseline strata are randomly downsampled. The result is a balanced design that protects subgroup-level statistical power for the smallest groups in the population.

§ 06 What this is not

TomoFair is a study proposal, not findings. The design has known constraints: a five-year timeline that may be outpaced by the technology it studies; a single-metro setting that may understate disparities in regions with less diverse provider workforces; within-hospital randomization that risks contamination across conditions; and self-reported demographic categories that compress meaningful within-group variation.

The contribution is methodological. It proposes a measurement instrument for a harm that is currently quantifiable only retrospectively, inconsistently, and at deployment scale.
